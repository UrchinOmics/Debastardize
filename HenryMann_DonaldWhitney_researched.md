### Henry Mann (1905–2000) and Donald Whitney (1915–2007)

**Methods:** Mann–Whitney U test (also known as the Wilcoxon rank-sum test, or the Wilcoxon–Mann–Whitney test).

Henry Berthold Mann was born on October 27, 1905, in Vienna, Austria-Hungary, to a Jewish family. His parents were Oscar and Friedrike (Schönnhof) Mann. He earned his Ph.D. in mathematics from the University of Vienna in 1935 under the supervision of Philipp Furtwängler, writing his dissertation in algebraic number theory.

After completing his doctorate, Mann spent a year teaching school in Vienna and a couple of years in research and tutoring. In March 1938, Nazi Germany annexed Austria in the Anschluss, and the consequences for Viennese Jews were immediate and devastating. Mann emigrated to the United States that year. He arrived in New York with no academic position and supported himself by tutoring students. After a series of temporary appointments, he joined the faculty at Ohio State University in 1946.

Before arriving at Ohio State, Mann had already produced one of his most celebrated results. In 1941, he proved the Schnirelmann–Landau conjecture in additive number theory. The technique he introduced in that proof, and its various modifications, led to further important results in the field. For this work, the American Mathematical Society awarded him the Cole Prize in Number Theory in 1946.

At Ohio State, one of Mann's first graduate students was Donald Ransom Whitney. Their collaboration produced the 1947 paper "On a Test of Whether One of Two Random Variables Is Stochastically Larger than the Other," published in the *Annals of Mathematical Statistics*. The paper extended and formalized the nonparametric rank-sum test that Frank Wilcoxon had introduced in a brief 1945 paper. Where Wilcoxon had proposed the method and provided only a few points of the distribution of his statistic, Mann and Whitney developed the full mathematical theory: they defined the U statistic, derived its exact distribution under the null hypothesis using a recurrence relation, and computed tables for samples up to size eight. The result is one of the most widely used nonparametric tests in science.

Mann also wrote the first mathematical book on the design of experiments, *Analysis and Design of Experiments* (1949). He remained at Ohio State until 1964, when he moved to a professorship at the U.S. Army's Mathematics Research Center at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, where he stayed until 1971. He then joined the University of Arizona, where he worked until his second retirement in 1975. Over a career of more than fifty years, he made significant contributions to algebra, number theory, statistics, and combinatorics. He died on February 1, 2000, in Tucson.

Donald Ransom Whitney was born on November 27, 1915, in Cleveland, Ohio, one of four children. He received his bachelor's degree from Oberlin College in 1936, where he met his future wife, Marian. He earned an M.A. in mathematics from Princeton University in 1939 and began teaching at Mary Washington College. From 1942 to 1946, he served in the U.S. Navy, teaching navigation to newly commissioned officers during the Second World War.

Upon his discharge in 1946, Whitney entered graduate school at Ohio State University, where his interest in statistics took root under the supervision of Henry Mann. He received his Ph.D. in 1948 — the Mann–Whitney paper having been published the year before, while he was still a doctoral student — and immediately joined the Ohio State mathematics faculty.

Whitney spent the rest of his career at Ohio State, and his contribution extended well beyond a single famous paper. He established the Statistics Laboratory, which provided consultation and assistance to faculty and students across the university. He personally assisted hundreds of scientists in many departments and colleges over the years. When the mathematics department needed leadership, Whitney served as acting chairman during a difficult period and was instrumental in recruiting Arnold Ross to chair the department in 1963, with Whitney serving as assistant chairman. By 1970, Whitney had gathered enough faculty and built enough infrastructure to establish a Division of Statistics within the mathematics department, complete with its own budget. In 1974, the Division became a full Department of Statistics, and Whitney served as its inaugural chairman for the first eight years.

Whitney was, in short, a builder of institutions. He took a statistical method developed in collaboration with his doctoral advisor and spent the rest of his career building the infrastructure — the lab, the courses, the department — that allowed an entire university to use statistics well. He died on August 16, 2007.

Mann was a Jewish refugee who fled the Nazis and rebuilt his career from nothing in a new country, producing landmark work in both pure mathematics and statistics. Whitney was a Navy veteran who became one of Mann's first students and devoted his life to making statistics accessible and useful at a major research university. Neither man's legacy carries the moral complications that attach to so many names in the history of statistics. Their test is one of the most commonly used tools in nonparametric statistics, and the story behind it is one of a refugee and his student doing excellent work.
