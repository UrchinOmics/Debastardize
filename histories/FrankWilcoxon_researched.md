### Frank Wilcoxon (1892–1965)

**Methods:** Wilcoxon rank-sum test (also known as the Mann–Whitney U test), Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Frank Wilcoxon was born on September 2, 1892, at Glengarriffe Castle near Cork, Ireland. He and a twin sister were the first children of wealthy American parents who had rented the castle during an extended European honeymoon. His father was a poet, outdoorsman, and hunter; the family home was in Catskill, New York.

Wilcoxon's early life was unconventional. Educated by private tutors as a child, he signed on as a crew member of a freighter in New York harbor around the age of sixteen. When the ship failed to sail after a week of chipping paint, he jumped ship — and, believing he had committed an offense comparable to desertion from the navy, fled to a remote area of West Virginia. He spent several years there manning an oil-well pump and later working as a tree surgeon. This wandering eventually led him to the Pennsylvania Military College, from which he graduated with a B.Sc. in 1917.

After serving at the Atlas Powder Company in Houghton, Michigan during World War I, Wilcoxon enrolled at Rutgers University in 1920 — where his contemporaries included the singer and activist Paul Robeson — and earned an M.S. in chemistry in 1921. He completed his Ph.D. in physical chemistry at Cornell University in 1924 and stayed on as a postdoctoral Heckscher Fellow through 1925. At Cornell he met Frederica Facius, an undergraduate, and they married on May 27, 1926. His twin sister had died in childbirth in 1918, a loss that affected him deeply and influenced his and Frederica's decision not to have children.

In 1925, Wilcoxon took a position at the Boyce Thompson Institute for Plant Research in Yonkers, New York, where he was assigned to study the use of copper compounds as fungicides. It was there that statistics found him. In late 1925 or early 1926, Wilcoxon, chemical engineer William John (Jack) Youden, and biologist F. E. Denny formed a study group to work through R. A. Fisher's newly published *Statistical Methods for Research Workers* (1925). The experience redirected both Wilcoxon's and Youden's careers. Wilcoxon's motivation was practical: he wanted something computationally simpler than the endless *t*-statistics his laboratory work required.

Wilcoxon remained at the Boyce Thompson Institute until 1941, then moved to the Atlas Powder Company, where he designed and directed the Control Laboratory. In 1943, he joined the American Cyanamid Company in Stamford, Connecticut, as group leader of the insecticide and fungicide laboratory. In 1950, he transferred to Cyanamid's Lederle Laboratories Division, where he built a statistical consulting group that served the company's research operations.

His landmark contribution came in a remarkably brief paper. "Individual Comparisons by Ranking Methods," published in *Biometrics Bulletin* in December 1945, ran just four pages. In it, Wilcoxon proposed two nonparametric tests — one for paired observations and one for unpaired — that replaced the assumption of normality required by Student's *t*-test with a simple ranking of observations. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test and the Wilcoxon rank-sum test gave researchers tools that worked without distributional assumptions, opening a branch of statistics — nonparametrics — that would grow enormously in the decades that followed. Henry Mann and Donald Whitney independently extended the rank-sum test in 1947, which is why the test is also known as the Mann–Whitney U test.

Over his career, Wilcoxon published more than seventy papers and co-authored (with Roberta A. Wilcox) *Some Rapid Approximate Statistical Procedures* (1949; revised 1964), a practical manual widely used in industrial research. He and Youden were early leaders in the Gordon Research Conference on Statistics in Chemistry and Chemical Engineering.

Wilcoxon retired from American Cyanamid in 1957. In 1960, he accepted a half-time appointment as Distinguished Lecturer in the Department of Statistics at Florida State University in Tallahassee. He continued teaching there until his death on November 18, 1965.

Wilcoxon was an industrial chemist who came to statistics because he needed better tools for his own data. He invented methods that millions of researchers use every day, and he did it in four pages, motivated by the straightforward desire to make laboratory analysis simpler and more honest.
