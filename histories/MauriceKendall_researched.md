### Maurice Kendall (1907–1983)

**Methods:** Kendall's tau (rank correlation coefficient), Kendall's W (coefficient of concordance), Kendall–Babington Smith tests for randomness, contributions to k-statistics.

Maurice George Kendall was born on September 6, 1907, in Kettering, Northamptonshire, the only child of John Roughton Kendall, an engineering worker, and Georgina Brewer. As a child he contracted cerebral meningitis — a disease that was almost always fatal at the time — and his survival was considered remarkable. His early interests were in languages, not mathematics.

Kendall's path to mathematics was not straightforward. He failed his Grammar School entrance exams and attended Derby Central School instead. Only near the end of his secondary schooling did he show aptitude for mathematics, and his headmaster arranged for him to transfer to the Grammar School for his final year. His mother supported his ambition to attend university over his father's preference that he train as an engineer. He won a scholarship to St John's College, Cambridge, where he read mathematics, played cricket and chess, and befriended the mathematician and polymath Jacob Bronowski. The two shared an interest in chess and played "negative chess," a variant in which the previous moves had to be deduced from a board position. Kendall graduated as a Mathematics Wrangler in 1929.

In 1930, he passed the Civil Service Examinations and joined the Ministry of Agriculture. It was during his years in the civil service that he taught himself statistics. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Statistical Society in 1934. In 1935, he met the aging statistician G. Udny Yule by chance, and in 1937 the two collaborated on a revision of Yule's standard textbook, *An Introduction to the Theory of Statistics*. The book, long known simply as "Yule and Kendall," trained generations of statisticians.

In 1938 and 1939, Kendall and Bernard Babington Smith tackled the problem of random number generation. They built one of the earliest mechanical devices for producing random digits — a cardboard disk divided into ten labeled sectors, spun at roughly 250 revolutions per minute, with a light beam flashing at random intervals of about two seconds. An observer recorded whichever digit the light illuminated. From this device they generated 100,000 random digits, published as *Tables of Random Sampling Numbers* (1939) — more than twice the number L. H. C. Tippett had published in 1927, and a standard reference until the RAND Corporation's *A Million Random Digits* appeared in 1955. More importantly, Kendall and Babington Smith formulated a series of tests for statistical randomness — including frequency tests and runs tests — that, with minor modifications, became widely used criteria for evaluating random number generators.

During this period Kendall also began developing the rank correlation coefficient that bears his name. Kendall's tau, first published in 1938, measures the ordinal association between two ranked variables. Unlike Spearman's rank correlation, which adapts the Pearson coefficient to ranks, Kendall's tau is based on concordant and discordant pairs of observations, giving it a natural probabilistic interpretation: it estimates the difference between the probability that two observations are in the same order and the probability that they are in the opposite order. His monograph *Rank Correlation* (1948) gave the method a comprehensive theoretical foundation.

In 1940, Kendall left the civil service to become statistician to the British Chamber of Shipping. Despite the constraints of wartime, he managed to produce the first volume of *The Advanced Theory of Statistics* in 1943 and the second in 1946. These volumes became one of the defining reference works in the field and remained in print for decades, later revised and expanded by Alan Stuart and J. Keith Ord. The book is still published as *Kendall's Advanced Theory of Statistics*.

In 1949, Kendall accepted the second chair of statistics at the London School of Economics. He held this post until 1961 and during this time published prolifically. His 1953 paper "The Analysis of Economic Time-Series, Part I: Prices" presented evidence that stock prices followed a near-random walk — a finding that anticipated the efficient-market hypothesis and influenced a generation of financial economists, though Kendall himself found the result puzzling rather than theoretically illuminating at the time. He also produced *Statistical Sources in the United Kingdom* (1952, 1957) and, with William Buckland, the *Dictionary of Statistical Terms* (1957), commissioned by the International Statistical Institute to standardize terminology across languages.

In 1961, Kendall left academia to become Managing Director — and later Chairman — of CEIR, a computer consultancy that became Scientific Control Systems (SCICON). It was an unusual move for a senior academic statistician, but Kendall had always been drawn to practical applications and organization.

When he retired from SCICON in 1972, he began what would become his most consequential organizational achievement. He was appointed Director of the World Fertility Survey, a joint project of the International Statistical Institute and the United Nations Fund for Population Activities. Kendall insisted on basing the project in London and oversaw what became the largest multinational sample survey ever undertaken at that time. Between 1973 and 1984, sixty-six countries conducted comparable surveys of human fertility, interviewing more than 341,000 women. The data transformed the study of global population dynamics and reproductive health.

Kendall was knighted in 1974 for his services to statistics. In 1980, the United Nations awarded him its Peace Medal for his work on the World Fertility Survey. He had previously received the Royal Statistical Society's Guy Medal in Silver (1945) and Guy Medal in Gold (1968), and was elected a Fellow of the British Academy. At the time of his death, he was honorary president of the International Statistical Institute.

Kendall was, by all accounts, a fair-minded and generous colleague. Alan Stuart, his longtime collaborator, described him as someone who shunned personal controversy, took pains to be fair to younger researchers, knew when and how to delegate, and wrote in a "transparent, balanced prose rarely achieved by scientists." He died on March 29, 1983, in Redhill, Surrey.
