### Howard Levene (1914–2003)

**Methods:** Levene's test for equality of variances; Levene's model in population genetics.

Howard Levene was born on January 17, 1914, in Brooklyn, New York. He graduated summa cum laude from New York University in 1941 and moved to Columbia University, where he initially worked on quality control for the war effort during World War II. He received his Ph.D. from Columbia in 1947 — the first doctorate awarded by what would become the Department of Statistics — and joined the faculty immediately after, remaining there as professor of mathematical statistics and genetics until his retirement in 1982.

Levene's career straddled two fields. In statistics, he is best known for Levene's test, proposed in 1960, which assesses the equality of variances across groups by applying an F-test to the absolute deviations of observations from their group means. The test's key innovation was its robustness to departures from normality, a limitation that plagued earlier methods like Bartlett's test. This made it a practical workhorse for checking the homogeneity-of-variance assumption that underlies many common statistical procedures, and it remains widely used today.

In population genetics, Levene collaborated extensively with Theodosius Dobzhansky on the landmark "Genetics of Natural Populations" series, providing the mathematical analysis for studies of natural selection in wild *Drosophila pseudoobscura* populations. His own major theoretical contribution, known as Levene's model (1953), was the first population genetic model to incorporate the existence of more than one ecological niche, demonstrating how spatial heterogeneity in selection could maintain genetic polymorphism. The model was foundational and generated a substantial body of follow-up work.

Levene was by all accounts an exceptional teacher. His statistics course in 1946–1947 counted among its students the future geneticist Ruth Sager and chronobiologist Colin Pittendrigh. Columbia's Department of Statistics named the Howard Levene Outstanding Teaching Award in his honor, given annually to graduate students since 1999. He served as president of the American Society of Naturalists in 1976.

Howard Levene died on July 2, 2003, at the age of 89. His memorial in the *Journal of Heredity* described a career defined by intellectual generosity and the rare ability to bridge the gap between mathematics and biology.
