### Karl Pearson (1857–1936)

**Methods:** Pearson correlation coefficient, Pearson's chi-squared test, principal component analysis (PCA), the Pearson distribution system, the method of moments, the p-value, the standard deviation (term and notation), the histogram (term), and the coefficient of variation. Co-founded the journal *Biometrika* (1901) and founded the *Annals of Eugenics* (1925).

Karl Pearson was one of the founders of modern statistics. His 1900 paper "On the Criterion that a Given System of Deviations from the Probable in the Case of a Correlated System of Variables is Such that it Can Be Reasonably Supposed to Have Arisen from Random Sampling" introduced the chi-squared goodness-of-fit test and, with it, the p-value — a concept that would come to dominate 20th-century science. His 1901 paper "On Lines and Planes of Closest Fit to Systems of Points in Space" laid the groundwork for principal component analysis. He developed the method of moments for parameter estimation (1902), formalized the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient (building on earlier work by Bravais and Galton), and created a system of continuous probability distributions to model skewed data. He standardized the term "standard deviation" and gave statistics much of its modern vocabulary. His eighteen-paper series *Mathematical Contributions to the Theory of Evolution* established the biometrical approach to heredity. He was, by any measure, one of the most consequential scientists of his era.

He was also a white supremacist who built the institutional architecture of British eugenics.

Pearson was a protege and biographer of Francis Galton, the man who coined the term "eugenics." When Galton died in 1911, he left his estate to University College London for a Chair of Eugenics. Pearson became the first Galton Professor of Eugenics, a position he held until 1933. He had already taken over Galton's Eugenics Record Office in 1907, renaming it the Galton Laboratory for National Eugenics. He ran the Department of Applied Statistics at UCL, which combined the Biometric Laboratory and the Galton Laboratory — fusing his statistical work with his eugenic mission. These were not separate endeavors. Pearson's statistical innovations were developed in the service of eugenics. The chi-squared test, significance testing, correlation — these tools emerged from his efforts to quantify racial difference and validate eugenic theory.

His 1900 lecture *National Life from the Standpoint of Science*, delivered at Newcastle, is a document of straightforward scientific racism. In it, Pearson declared: "History shows me one way, and one way only, in which a high state of civilization has been produced, namely the struggle of race with race, and the survival of the physically and mentally fitter race." He dismissed entire peoples: "How many centuries, how many thousands of years, have the Kaffir or the negro held large districts in Africa undisturbed by the white man? Yet their intertribal struggles have not yet produced a civilization in the least comparable with the Aryan. Educate and nurture them as you will, I do not believe that you will succeed in modifying the stock." He celebrated the colonization of America as progress, arguing that "in place of the red man, contributing practically nothing to the work and thought of the world, we have a great nation." He called it "weak humanitarianism" to regret the replacement of non-white peoples by white settlers.

Pearson argued that the nation must be "a homogeneous whole, not a mixture of superior and inferior races," and that "every new land we colonize with white men is a source of strength." He described the suffering caused by racial competition as having "redeeming features" because it ensured the survival of the "fitter race."

In 1925, Pearson founded the journal *Annals of Eugenics* and used its inaugural issue to publish a study — co-authored with Margaret Moul — titled "The Problem of Alien Immigration into Great Britain, Illustrated by an Examination of Russian and Polish Jewish Children." The paper argued that Jewish immigrants were "somewhat inferior physically and mentally to the native population" and warned that they "will develop into a parasitic race." This was not a dispassionate scientific finding. It was a deliberate effort by one of the world's most prominent statisticians to provide a scientific basis for restricting Jewish immigration to Britain.

Pearson advocated for what he called "practical eugenics": policies to ensure that the nation was "substantially recruited from the better stocks." He called for discouraging reproduction among "the unthrifty ... the mentally defective" and "the criminal, the tramp and the congenital pauper." He described Britain as a "crowded island" being "swamped" by "undesirable" groups. His institutional influence helped make eugenics a mainstream scientific enterprise in early 20th-century Britain, and his methods and prestige were readily adopted by American eugenicists who used them to lobby for forced sterilization laws and restrictive immigration policies.

In June 2020, UCL formally apologized for its history and legacy of eugenics. The university renamed the Pearson Building (now the North-West Wing) and the Pearson Lecture Theatre (now Lecture Theatre G22), along with spaces named after Galton.
