### Bernard Welch (1911–1989)

**Bernard Lewis Welch.** Born 1911 in Sunderland, County Durham; died 29 December 1989.

**Methods:** Welch's t-test, Welch's ANOVA, Welch–Satterthwaite equation for approximate degrees of freedom.

Bernard Welch was the youngest of four brothers, raised in Sunderland and educated at the Bede School. He went to Brasenose College, Oxford, where he captained the college cricket team for two years and graduated with a first-class degree in mathematics in 1933. He then moved to University College London to study statistics at a time when Karl Pearson and R.A. Fisher were building it into a center for statistical inference and biological applications. Welch made his own distinctive theoretical contributions there and committed himself to extending the reach of statistics into industrial and agricultural work.

His most lasting contribution came from tackling the Behrens–Fisher problem: how to compare two sample means when the population variances are unequal. His 1938 paper in *Biometrika* proposed the test statistic and an approximation for the degrees of freedom that became Welch's t-test. His 1947 paper in the same journal, "The Generalization of 'Student's' Problem When Several Different Population Variances Are Involved," extended the approach to handle multiple populations, forming the basis of what is now called Welch's ANOVA. The degrees-of-freedom approximation he developed is also known as the Welch–Satterthwaite equation. These methods are now the default in most statistical software for comparing means when equal variances cannot be assumed.

From 1939 to 1946, Welch served as a Scientific Officer on the Ordnance Board of the Ministry of Supply, contributing statistical expertise to the British war effort during the Second World War. After the war he took up a Readership in Statistics in the Department of Mathematics at the University of Leeds, then one of the few British universities with a statistician on its mathematical staff. He was appointed to the Chair in Statistics in 1968 and delivered an inaugural lecture titled *The Biometricians and the Development of Statistics*, a historical account of the field's origins. When the School of Mathematics established a separate Department of Statistics, Welch became its first head, serving until his retirement in 1976.

Welch was a founder of the Industrial and Agricultural Research Section of the Royal Statistical Society, reflecting his lifelong interest in the practical applications of statistical methods beyond the academy. He suffered a stroke in June 1989 and died on 29 December of that year.

There is no evidence that Welch was involved in eugenics advocacy, discriminatory practices, or other harmful activities. He was, by all available accounts, a working statistician who solved an important practical problem and spent his career building a department and advancing the application of statistics in industry and agriculture.
