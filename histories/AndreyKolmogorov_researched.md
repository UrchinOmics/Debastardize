### Andrey Kolmogorov (1903–1987)

**Methods:** Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, Kolmogorov's axioms of probability, Kolmogorov complexity, Chapman–Kolmogorov equations, Kolmogorov–Arnold–Moser (KAM) theorem, Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem, Kolmogorov backward/forward equations, Kolmogorov's zero–one law.

Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov was born on April 25, 1903, in Tambov, Russia. His mother died giving birth to him. He was raised by his maternal aunt, Vera Yakovlena, for whom he held deep affection throughout his life. He entered Moscow State University in 1920, and by 1922 — at age nineteen — had constructed a Fourier series that diverges almost everywhere, a result that brought him immediate international recognition.

Kolmogorov's contributions to mathematics are staggering in both breadth and depth. His 1933 monograph *Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung* ("Foundations of the Theory of Probability") established the axiomatic framework on which all of modern probability theory rests. Three axioms — non-negativity, normalization, and countable additivity — gave probability its rigorous mathematical footing. The Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, developed in the 1930s with Nikolai Smirnov, remains one of the most widely used nonparametric tests for comparing distributions. His work on stochastic processes, particularly Markov processes, produced the Chapman–Kolmogorov equations and the Kolmogorov backward and forward equations, foundational tools in the study of random systems evolving over time.

Beyond probability and statistics, Kolmogorov made landmark contributions to topology (defining cohomology groups and the cohomology ring), turbulence theory (his 1941 papers remain central to the field), classical mechanics (the KAM theorem on the persistence of quasiperiodic motions, presented at the 1954 International Congress of Mathematicians), algorithmic information theory (Kolmogorov complexity, published in 1963), and the resolution of Hilbert's thirteenth problem (with his student Vladimir Arnold, in 1957). He worked across so many fields that a full accounting of his contributions is itself a substantial exercise.

Kolmogorov's personal life was defined by a close partnership with the topologist Pavel Alexandrov. The two met during a boat trip on the Volga in 1929 and shared a house in the village of Komarovka outside Moscow from 1935 until Alexandrov's death in 1982. Alexandrov described the friendship as lasting fifty years without a single quarrel. Several historians have concluded that the relationship was romantic, though neither man acknowledged this publicly — unsurprising given that homosexuality was criminalized in the Soviet Union under Article 121 of the penal code from 1933 onward.

That criminalization had consequences beyond their private lives. During the Luzin affair of 1936, the Soviet mathematical establishment turned on Nikolai Luzin, Kolmogorov's and Alexandrov's former teacher, accusing him of plagiarism and ideological disloyalty. Both Kolmogorov and Alexandrov testified against Luzin at hearings held between July 7 and July 15, 1936. Some researchers have argued that Soviet authorities pressured the two men by threatening to expose their relationship. Others, notably the mathematician S. S. Kutateladze, have argued on the basis of archival documents that Luzin's former students acted out of genuine personal grievance rather than state coercion. Luzin was convicted by the commission but was neither expelled from the Academy nor arrested, though he lost his official positions.

Kolmogorov also stood on the right side of one of the twentieth century's most destructive episodes of political interference in science. In the 1930s and 1940s, the agronomist Trofim Lysenko, backed by Stalin, imposed a pseudoscientific rejection of Mendelian genetics on Soviet biology — with catastrophic consequences for both Soviet agriculture and the scientists who opposed him. Kolmogorov, who had long-standing interests in biology and had published on theoretical ecology and population genetics, used his mathematical expertise to challenge Lysenko's claims. This was not without risk. Genetics was effectively banned in the Soviet Union after the August 1948 session of the Lenin All-Union Academy of Agricultural Sciences, and many geneticists lost their positions or worse.

Later in life, Kolmogorov devoted substantial energy to mathematics education. In 1963, he helped establish specialized mathematics-physics boarding schools attached to major Soviet universities, beginning with the school at Moscow State University that now bears his name. In the 1970s, he led a broader reform of the Soviet mathematics curriculum for grades 4 through 10. The reform was ambitious — too ambitious, by many accounts — and its implementation was troubled, but it reflected Kolmogorov's genuine commitment to bringing rigorous mathematical thinking to a wider audience.

Kolmogorov was elected a Foreign Member of the Royal Society in 1964. The Soviet Union awarded him seven Orders of Lenin, the Order of the October Revolution, and the title Hero of Socialist Labour, along with Lenin Prizes and State Prizes. He died in Moscow on October 20, 1987.
