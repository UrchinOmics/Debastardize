### Charles Spearman (1863–1945)

**Methods:** Spearman's rank correlation coefficient, Spearman–Brown prediction formula, factor analysis, the *g* factor (general intelligence), Spearman's hypothesis.

Charles Edward Spearman was born in London on September 10, 1863. He joined the British Army as a regular officer of engineers in 1883 and served for fifteen years, including the Burma campaign, rising to the rank of major. In 1897, at age 34, he resigned his commission to study experimental psychology in Germany under Wilhelm Wundt at the University of Leipzig. His studies were interrupted when the army recalled him during the Second Boer War; he served as Deputy Assistant Adjutant General in Guernsey from 1900 before returning to Leipzig to complete his doctorate in 1906.

In 1904, Spearman published "'General Intelligence,' Objectively Determined and Measured" in the *American Journal of Psychology*. This paper introduced factor analysis as a statistical method and proposed that performance on diverse cognitive tests reflects a single underlying general factor, which Spearman called *g*. The paper was foundational. Factor analysis became one of the most widely used techniques in psychology and the social sciences, and the *g* factor became the central construct in psychometric research on intelligence. Spearman's rank correlation coefficient, a nonparametric measure of the strength of association between two ranked variables, remains a standard tool in statistics. The Spearman–Brown prediction formula, developed with William Brown in 1910, estimates the reliability of a test after changing its length.

In 1907, Spearman was appointed Reader in Experimental Psychology at University College London, succeeding William McDougall. He became Grote Professor of Philosophy of Mind and Logic in 1911 and held that chair until his retirement in 1931. His department became the origin of what Arthur Jensen later called the "London School" of differential psychology — a tradition focused on individual differences, psychometrics, and the heritability of intelligence. Spearman's most prominent student and successor at University College London was Cyril Burt, who was later found to have fabricated data in twin studies used to argue for the heritability of intelligence. Other students included Raymond Cattell, who went on to co-found the Society for Multivariate Experimental Psychology and who late in life helped fund the explicitly white-supremacist *Mankind Quarterly*. Spearman was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1924.

Spearman was a committed eugenicist. He was a Fellow of the Eugenics Society of London (later renamed the Galton Institute). In 1912, in a paper co-authored with Bernard Hart titled "General Ability, Its Existence and Nature," he wrote: "An accurate measurement of everyone's intelligence would seem to herald the feasibility of selecting the better endowed persons for admission into citizenship — and even for the right of having offspring." He repeated this sentiment in his 1927 book *The Abilities of Man: Their Nature and Measurement*, writing that one could conceive "the establishment of a minimum index to qualify for parliamentary vote, and above all, for the right to have offspring." These were not passing remarks. Spearman saw intelligence testing as a tool for sorting people, and he said so plainly.

Spearman also laid groundwork that others used to construct scientific-racist arguments about racial differences in intelligence. In *The Abilities of Man*, he commented on observed differences in test scores between Black and white populations and suggested these differences were related to *g*. Arthur Jensen formalized this observation in 1985 as "Spearman's hypothesis" — the claim that the magnitude of Black–white score gaps on cognitive tests correlates with the tests' loading on *g*. Jensen, and later J. Philippe Rushton, used this hypothesis to argue for innate racial differences in intelligence, a conclusion rejected by the scientific consensus. The hypothesis bears Spearman's name because Jensen credited Spearman's 1927 observations as its origin.

The British Psychological Society awarded its Spearman Medal annually from 1971 to early-career researchers for outstanding published work. In 2021, the Society retired the award, citing concern over Spearman's links to the eugenics movement. The BPS also hosted a symposium and exhibition, "We Are Not Alone: Legacies of Eugenics," examining the role of psychologists including Spearman and Burt in promoting eugenics in Britain.

Spearman died on September 17, 1945, in London. He was 83 years old and in poor health. He ended his own life by jumping from an upper-story window of the University of London Hospital.
