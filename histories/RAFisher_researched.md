### R.A. Fisher (1890–1962)

**Methods:** Analysis of variance (ANOVA), Fisher's exact test, the F-distribution (named after him by George Snedecor), maximum likelihood estimation, the method of sufficiency, Fisher information, Fisher's linear discriminant, fiducial inference, the concept of the null hypothesis, randomization in experimental design, Fisher's z-transformation for correlation coefficients, Fisher's method for combining p-values. Author of *Statistical Methods for Research Workers* (1925) and *The Design of Experiments* (1935), two of the most influential statistical textbooks of the twentieth century. Editor of the *Annals of Eugenics* (1934–1952).

Ronald Aylmer Fisher is widely regarded as the single most important figure in the history of statistics. His 1922 paper "On the Mathematical Foundations of Theoretical Statistics" introduced maximum likelihood estimation and the concepts of sufficiency, efficiency, and consistency — the theoretical foundations on which modern statistical inference rests. His development of analysis of variance (ANOVA) at Rothamsted Experimental Station in the 1920s revolutionized agricultural science and experimental design. His 1925 textbook *Statistical Methods for Research Workers* standardized the practice of significance testing and made the p < 0.05 threshold a near-universal convention. His 1935 *The Design of Experiments* formalized the principles of randomization, replication, and blocking that remain the basis of experimental methodology. His 1918 paper "The Correlation Between Relatives on the Supposition of Mendelian Inheritance" reconciled the biometric and Mendelian schools of genetics, founding the field of population genetics. His 1930 book *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* provided the first rigorous mathematical framework for Darwinian evolution, including the fundamental theorem of natural selection.

Fisher was a eugenicist from the beginning of his career to the end.

In 1911, at the age of twenty-one, Fisher became the founding chairman of the University of Cambridge Eugenics Society. Other founding members included John Maynard Keynes, R.C. Punnett, and Horace Darwin. At the society's inaugural meeting on November 10, 1911, Fisher read a paper titled "Heredity" that outlined the intellectual program he would pursue for the rest of his life: the fusion of evolutionary biology, genetics, and statistics in the service of human improvement. During World War I, he volunteered to review books for the *Eugenics Review*, the journal of the Eugenics Education Society, and was eventually hired for a part-time position with the publication.

*The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* (1930) is often cited as Fisher's masterwork in evolutionary biology. What is less often mentioned is that five of its twelve chapters — over a hundred pages — are devoted to eugenics. In these chapters, Fisher argued that civilizations decline because their upper classes fail to reproduce at sufficient rates. Using data from the 1911 British census, he documented an inverse relationship between social class and fertility and proposed that this "dysgenic" pattern explained the fall of ancient Egypt, Babylon, Greece, and Rome. His proposed solution was a system of family allowances proportional to the father's earnings, designed to make it financially feasible for the professional and upper classes to have larger families and thereby restore what he saw as the nation's declining genetic stock. Recent scholarship has shown that much of this eugenic material was drafted as early as 1919 — before the evolutionary genetics chapters were written — suggesting that Fisher's eugenic convictions were not a secondary interest but the conceptual foundation for the scientific work that followed.

In 1930, Fisher wrote a four-page pamphlet titled "The Elimination of Mental Defect" for the Committee for Legalizing Sterilization, an arm of the Eugenics Education Society. Between 1929 and 1934, the Society campaigned for legislation permitting the sterilization of people classified as "mental defectives." In 1932, the British government established a committee under Laurence Brock to investigate the question. Fisher served on the Brock Committee as a representative of the Eugenics Society and contributed detailed annotations and scientific advice to the testimony prepared by Carlos Blacker, the Society's General Secretary. The Brock Committee unanimously recommended the legalization of voluntary sterilization for persons deemed "mentally defective," suffering from "severe mental disorder," or believed to carry "a grave disability which has been shown to be transmissible." The recommendations were never enacted.

In 1933, Fisher succeeded Karl Pearson as the Galton Professor of Eugenics at University College London and head of the Galton Laboratory. He held the position until 1943, when he moved to Cambridge as the Arthur Balfour Professor of Genetics. During his time at UCL, he served as editor of the *Annals of Eugenics*, a journal Pearson had founded in 1925. Fisher edited the journal from 1934 to 1952.

In 1947, Fisher began corresponding with Otmar Freiherr von Verschuer, a German geneticist and leading figure in Nazi racial hygiene. Verschuer had been the doctoral supervisor of Josef Mengele, the SS physician who conducted lethal experiments on prisoners at Auschwitz, including experiments on twins. In a 1944 report to the German Research Council, Verschuer had acknowledged receiving "scientific materials" from Auschwitz supplied by Mengele — including blood samples taken from twins whom Mengele had deliberately infected with typhus, and in some cases samples obtained by murdering them. In a letter to Verschuer dated February 3, 1948, Fisher wrote: "It does not seem to be at all easy to arrange a visit to this country. There has evidently been a good deal of denigration, which I do not believe has any substantial basis." Later that year, on November 19, 1948, Fisher wrote a reference letter on Verschuer's behalf to Karl Wezler, Dean of the Medical Faculty at the University of Frankfurt. In it, Fisher stated:

> It was, I think, his misfortune rather than his fault that racial theory was a part of the Nazi ideology... In spite of their prejudices I have no doubt also that the Party sincerely wished to benefit the German racial stock, especially by the elimination of manifest defectives, such as those deficient mentally, and I do not doubt that von Verschuer gave, as I should have done, his support to such a movement.

Fisher wrote this in 1948. The Nuremberg trials had concluded in 1946. The Doctors' trial, which prosecuted Nazi physicians for human experimentation, ended in August 1947. The scale and nature of Nazi atrocities — including forced sterilization, the T4 euthanasia program that murdered over 70,000 disabled people, and the systematic extermination of millions — were extensively documented and publicly known. Fisher described the "elimination of manifest defectives" as a movement he himself would have supported.

In 1950, UNESCO convened a panel of experts to draft a statement on race, motivated by the crimes of the Nazi regime. The resulting "Statement by Experts on Race Problems" concluded that there was no scientific basis for claims of racial superiority or inferiority in intellectual or emotional capacity. Fisher was among a small group of scientists — alongside Cyril Darlington, Carleton Coon, and Giuseppe Genna — who formally dissented. In a letter to UNESCO dated October 3, 1951, published in *The Race Concept: Results of an Inquiry* (1952), Fisher wrote that human racial groups differ "in their innate capacity for intellectual and emotional development, seeing that such groups do differ undoubtedly in a very large number of their genes." He argued that "the practical international problem is that of learning to share the resources of this planet amicably with persons of materially different nature" and that this problem was "being obscured by entirely well intentioned efforts to minimise the real differences that exist."

In 1956, Fisher became a paid scientific consultant to the Tobacco Manufacturers' Standing Committee. At the time, the evidence linking cigarette smoking to lung cancer was mounting rapidly. The British Doctors' Study, led by Richard Doll and Austin Bradford Hill, had published its initial results in 1954, showing a strong dose-response relationship between smoking and lung cancer mortality. Fisher rejected the causal interpretation. In a series of letters to *Nature* in 1957 and 1958 — including "Lung Cancer and Cigarettes?" (July 12, 1958) and "Cancer and Smoking" (August 30, 1958) — and in a 1959 pamphlet titled *Smoking: The Cancer Controversy*, Fisher argued that the association between smoking and cancer could be explained by a shared genetic predisposition: a genotype that made people both more likely to smoke and more susceptible to lung cancer. To investigate this hypothesis, he contacted Verschuer for twin data. Fisher's genetic confounding hypothesis was scientifically untenable given the totality of the evidence available, and his analysis, as epidemiologist Paul Stolley later documented, was "flawed by an unwillingness to examine the entire body of data available and prematurely drawn conclusions." The tobacco industry seized on Fisher's reputation and his genetic hypothesis as centerpieces of their public relations campaign to manufacture doubt about the link between smoking and cancer — a campaign that delayed public health action and cost millions of lives.

Fisher was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1929 and was knighted in 1952. He spent his final years in Adelaide, Australia, where he died on July 29, 1962.

In June 2020, Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge removed a stained-glass window commemorating Fisher's 7x7 Latin Square from the college hall. The window had been installed in 1989. The College Council stated that its decision was made in light of Fisher's "endorsement of eugenics." That same month, the Committee of Presidents of Statistical Societies (COPSS) retired the R.A. Fisher Award and Lectureship, renaming it the COPSS Distinguished Achievement Award and Lectureship.
