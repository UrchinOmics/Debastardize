### John Tukey (1915–2000)

**Methods:** Tukey's Honest Significant Difference (HSD) test, Tukey lambda distribution, Tukey test of additivity, box-and-whisker plot, stem-and-leaf display, Cooley–Tukey Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithm.

John Wilder Tukey was born on June 16, 1915, in New Bedford, Massachusetts, the only child of Ralph H. Tukey, a Latin teacher, and Adah Tasker Tukey, a private tutor. His parents educated him largely at home using a Socratic method — responding to his questions with clues and further questions rather than direct answers. He attended regular classes only for subjects like French. The approach worked: he made heavy use of New Bedford's public library and arrived at Brown University already formidably well-read.

Tukey earned a B.A. in chemistry from Brown in 1936 and an M.S. in chemistry in 1937. He entered Princeton that fall intending to continue in chemistry but switched to mathematics, completing his Ph.D. in just two years under Solomon Lefschetz with a dissertation on topology, published as *Convergence and Uniformity in Topology* (1940).

During World War II, Tukey worked at Princeton's Fire Control Research Office, where he collaborated with Samuel Wilks and William Cochran on problems of ballistics and artillery control. The war directed his attention permanently toward statistics. After the war, he returned to Princeton's mathematics faculty and in 1945 also joined AT&T Bell Laboratories — beginning a dual appointment he would maintain for decades. He became a full professor at Princeton by age 35. In 1965, he founded Princeton's Department of Statistics and chaired it until 1970. At Bell Labs, he rose to associate executive director for research in the statistics and data analysis division.

Tukey's scientific output was vast and varied. His most widely known contribution, developed with James Cooley in 1965, was the Fast Fourier Transform algorithm, which reduced the computation of discrete Fourier transforms by orders of magnitude. The Cooley–Tukey FFT remains fundamental to signal processing, astrophysics, medical imaging, and virtually any field that works with waveforms. He also pioneered modern spectral analysis for time series data.

In statistics proper, Tukey's influence was equally deep. His 1977 book *Exploratory Data Analysis* challenged the prevailing emphasis on confirmatory hypothesis testing and argued that researchers should look at their data carefully and flexibly before reaching for formal tests. He introduced the box-and-whisker plot and popularized the stem-and-leaf display — tools still taught in introductory statistics courses. His work on robust estimation developed methods that performed well even when standard distributional assumptions did not hold. Tukey's HSD test, a method for making pairwise comparisons after an analysis of variance while controlling the family-wise error rate, remains one of the most commonly used post-hoc tests in science.

Tukey coined two words that outlasted most of his statistical vocabulary. In 1947, working at Bell Labs on early computing problems, he introduced the term "bit" as a contraction of "binary digit." In 1958, in an article in *The American Mathematical Monthly*, he used the word "software" — its first known appearance in print — to describe the programs that ran on computing hardware.

Beyond academia and Bell Labs, Tukey was deeply involved in government advisory work. He contributed to classified projects during the Cold War, reportedly including work related to the U-2 spy plane program and uranium enrichment. He served as a U.S. representative at nuclear disarmament conferences, where the statistical problem of verifying compliance with test ban treaties drew on exactly his kind of expertise. In 1954, with William Cochran and Frederick Mosteller, he authored a rigorous critique of the Kinsey Report's methodology for the American Statistical Association, arguing that Kinsey's non-random sampling made his conclusions unreliable — and reportedly telling Kinsey that a random sample of three people would have been more informative than Kinsey's hundreds of self-selected respondents. In the 1970s, he chaired a National Academy of Sciences committee on the effect of aerosol sprays on the ozone layer. From 1960 to 1980, he designed the statistical models behind NBC's television election-night projections.

On the politically charged question of census adjustment, Tukey came down on the side of statistical correction. In the litigation following the 1990 Census, he testified in favor of adjusting the raw count using statistical methods to correct for the differential undercount of Black and Hispanic residents in urban areas — a position opposed by the Commerce Department and many Republican lawmakers who argued that the Constitution required an "actual enumeration." Tukey maintained that the adjusted count would contain fewer errors than the unadjusted one.

Tukey married Elizabeth Louise Rapp on July 19, 1950. She had been the personnel director of the Educational Testing Service in Princeton and later became an antiques dealer. They had no children. Elizabeth died on January 6, 1998. Tukey himself died of a heart attack on July 26, 2000, in New Brunswick, New Jersey.

Colleagues described him as an exuberant, gregarious presence — a large man who loved folk dancing, collected murder mysteries and science fiction, and once defined a statistician as someone who could lie flat on a table and reel off scheduling solutions from memory. He proposed that the collective noun for a group of statisticians should be "a quarrel." His teaching style was deliberately oblique; he wanted students to work things out for themselves. His friendship with Richard Feynman produced a famous anecdote about counting: Feynman counted time by reciting numbers internally and could read while counting but not speak; Tukey visualized a number tape running past and could speak while counting but not read. The difference illustrated, for both of them, the variety of internal representations the mind can use for the same task.

Tukey received the S. S. Wilks Award (1965), the National Medal of Science from President Nixon (1973), and the IEEE Medal of Honor (1982). He was a member of the National Academy of Sciences and a foreign member of the Royal Society.

John Tukey was a statistician who looked at data the way a naturalist looks at a landscape — with curiosity before theory. He gave researchers tools for seeing what was actually in front of them, coined the vocabulary of the digital age, and spent his career insisting that the right way to do science was to pay attention first and formalize later.
