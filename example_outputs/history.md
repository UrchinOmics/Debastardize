# History of Eponymous Methods in This Project

This file documents the people whose names appear as eponyms in the statistical methods and algorithms used in this codebase. It is a factual record — not an apology, not a disclaimer. Every eponymous method has a human behind it, and this is who they were.

---

**Yoav Benjamini (b. 1949)** — Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction. Israeli statistician at Tel Aviv University. Co-developed the FDR framework with Yosef Hochberg in their 1995 paper, one of the most cited statistical papers ever written. Winner of the Israel Prize (2012) and the Rousseeuw Prize for Statistics (2024).

**Carlo Emilio Bonferroni (1892-1960)** — Bonferroni correction (referenced conceptually; not directly called in this codebase). Italian mathematician who studied under Giuseppe Peano at the University of Turin. Served as rector of the University of Bari during the 1920s-1930s under Mussolini's fascist government, a period when all Italian university professors were required to swear an oath of allegiance to the regime. No documented evidence of active ideological commitment beyond the systemic baseline^1.

**R.A. Fisher (1890-1962)** — Fisher's exact test. Fisher made foundational contributions to statistics and experimental design. He served as Head of the Department of Eugenics at University College London from 1933, edited the *Annals of Eugenics*, campaigned for the legalization of eugenic sterilization, and was one of three scientists to formally object to UNESCO's 1950 anti-racism statement^2,3.

**Yosef Hochberg (1945-2013)** — Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction (co-namesake). Israeli statistician at Tel Aviv University. Co-developed the FDR procedure and separately developed Hochberg's step-up procedure for controlling family-wise error rate.

**Andrey Kolmogorov (1903-1987)** — Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. One of the greatest mathematicians of the 20th century, responsible for the axiomatic foundations of probability theory. Quietly protected Jewish researchers during Soviet antisemitism and founded schools for mathematically gifted children.

**Karl Pearson (1857-1936)** — Pearson correlation coefficient. Pearson was the first Galton Professor of Eugenics at University College London and founded the *Annals of Eugenics* in 1925. He wrote that national progress comes "by way of war with inferior races" and campaigned for a "homogeneous white race"^4,5.

**Georges Seurat (1859-1891)** — Seurat R package (named after the painter). Post-Impressionist painter who developed Pointillism. The single-cell analysis package uses his name as a metaphor: individual cells as individual dots composing a biological picture.

**Nikolai Smirnov (1900-1966)** — Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (co-namesake). Soviet mathematician who computed and tabulated the critical values for the KS test, making it practically usable.

**Charles Spearman (1863-1945)** — Spearman's rank correlation. British psychologist who developed the concept of general intelligence (the *g* factor) and factor analysis. While not a eugenics campaigner, his *g* factor framework became central to eugenic arguments about racial and class hierarchies in intelligence^6,7.

**Conrad Hal Waddington (1905-1975)** — WOT (Waddington Optimal Transport). British developmental biologist who coined the term "epigenetics" and developed the metaphor of the epigenetic landscape. Signatory of the 1939 Geneticists' Manifesto, which explicitly denounced Nazi racism and racial theories of genetic superiority.

**Frank Wilcoxon (1892-1965)** — Wilcoxon rank-sum test, Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Born in Ireland to American parents, Wilcoxon was an industrial chemist who developed nonparametric statistical tests for practical use in biological assay. His 1945 paper introducing both tests was a landmark in distribution-free inference.
