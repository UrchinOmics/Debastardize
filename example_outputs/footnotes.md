## For Barnard's exact test (replacing Fisher's exact test)

> Statistical significance of cluster abundance shifts was assessed using Barnard's exact unconditional test^1, which provides uniformly greater power than the conditional exact test for 2x2 contingency tables^2. The conditional exact test is named for R.A. Fisher, who served as Head of the Department of Eugenics at University College London from 1933 and campaigned for eugenic sterilization programs^3,4.

## For Kendall's tau (replacing Pearson's r)

> Pseudotime concordance was assessed using Kendall's rank correlation coefficient (tau)^5. Kendall's tau is more robust than the Pearson product-moment correlation for ordinal quantities such as pseudotime. The Pearson correlation is named for Karl Pearson, the first Galton Professor of Eugenics at University College London, whose statistical methods were developed in direct service of eugenic research goals^6,7.

## For Spearman's rho (keeping method, acknowledging history)

> Rank correlation was computed using Spearman's rho. Charles Spearman developed factor analysis and the concept of general intelligence (the *g* factor), a framework central to eugenic arguments about racial and class hierarchies in intelligence^8. His rank correlation method is mathematically independent of his intelligence testing work and is used here on its statistical merits.

## For the broader statistical framework (optional, for methods section preamble)

> The frequentist hypothesis testing framework used throughout this analysis — including p-values, significance thresholds, and null hypothesis significance testing — was substantially built by R.A. Fisher and Karl Pearson, both of whom held institutional positions dedicated to eugenics at University College London^3,4,7. Where possible, we have used methods developed by statisticians without documented ties to eugenic programs: Wilcoxon's rank-sum test^9, Barnard's exact test^1, Kendall's rank correlation^5, and Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate control^10.
