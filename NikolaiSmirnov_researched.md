### Nikolai Smirnov (1900–1966)

**Methods:** Kolmogorov–Smirnov test (two-sample version), Cramér–von Mises–Smirnov criterion, theory of limit distributions of order statistics.

Nikolai Vasilyevich Smirnov was born on October 17, 1900, in Moscow, into the family of a church clerk who also worked in the office of the Bolshoi Theater. He completed his gymnasium education during the First World War, during which he served in various medical units of the military.

Smirnov graduated from the Faculty of Physics and Mathematics at Moscow State University and, beginning in 1926, taught mathematics at the Timiryazev Agricultural Academy, the Moscow City Pedagogical Institute, and Moscow State University. Over this period he narrowed his research to probability theory and mathematical statistics.

In 1938, Smirnov defended his doctoral dissertation, "On approximation of the distribution laws of random variables," which laid the groundwork for his later contributions to nonparametric statistics. That same year he joined the Steklov Institute of Mathematics of the Academy of Sciences of the USSR, where he would work for the rest of his life.

Smirnov's most important contribution came in 1939, when he published "Estimate of deviation between empirical distribution functions in two independent samples" in the *Bulletin of Moscow University*. The paper introduced a nonparametric test for comparing two independent samples by defining a test statistic as the maximum difference between their empirical distribution functions. This extended Andrey Kolmogorov's 1933 one-sample goodness-of-fit test to the two-sample case, producing what is now known as the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test — one of the most widely used nonparametric tests in applied statistics. Smirnov also contributed to the development of the Cramér–von Mises–Smirnov criterion, another nonparametric goodness-of-fit test.

Beyond the KS test, Smirnov was a founder of the theory of limit distributions of order statistics. His 1949 monograph "Limit distributions for the terms of a variational series," published in the *Trudy* of the Steklov Institute, was a major contribution to this field. In the 1950s, he was among the first Soviet mathematicians to compile modern manuals on the practical application of statistical methods in engineering and the natural sciences. Together with L. N. Bol'shev, he produced a widely used series of tables of special functions for probability theory and mathematical statistics. These textbooks and tables were used throughout the USSR and abroad.

In 1957, Smirnov was appointed Head of Mathematical Statistics at the Steklov Institute. He received the Stalin Prize in 1951 and the Order of the Red Banner of Labour in 1953. In 1960, he was elected a Corresponding Member of the Academy of Sciences of the USSR.

In his final years, Smirnov returned to mathematical genetics, a field he intended to contribute to with several publications. His sudden death on June 2, 1966, in Moscow, left this work unfinished.

Smirnov was a working mathematician who spent his career at Soviet research institutions, building the theoretical foundations of nonparametric statistics and making those methods accessible to practicing scientists and engineers. No record of controversy or harmful activities is associated with his life or work.
