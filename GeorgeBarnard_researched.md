### George Barnard (1915–2002)

**Methods:** Barnard's exact test (unconditional exact test for 2×2 contingency tables), sequential testing methods, contributions to the likelihood principle, concept of the pivotal quantity in inference, introduction of conjugate prior distributions in Bayesian statistics.

George Alfred Barnard was born on 23 September 1915 in Walthamstow, in London's East End. His father was a cabinet maker and his mother had been a domestic servant. He won a scholarship to St John's College, Cambridge, where he read mathematics, and then received a fellowship to study at Princeton from 1937 to 1939, where he worked primarily on mathematical logic.

Barnard was a committed anti-fascist and left-wing activist from a young age. He joined the Communist Party of Great Britain in 1933, while still a teenager, and participated in anti-fascist marches in the East End of London. A fellow activist remembered him as a "lean-and-hungry-looking mathematician from a working class family" who served as the party's chief local commissar among students. His political convictions were not merely theoretical: when he took a job as a mathematical consultant at Plessey, the engineering firm, in 1940, he was also an active shop steward. His Communist Party membership and left-wing activity at Princeton resulted in him being denied a U.S. visa for many years after the war.

The Second World War made Barnard a statistician, as it did for many mathematicians of his generation. In 1942 he joined the Ministry of Supply as head of a specialist research group, SR17, applying quality control and sampling methods to military production. It was there that he developed sequential methods of testing independently of — and roughly in parallel with — Abraham Wald at Columbia and Alan Turing at Bletchley Park. His 1945 letter to *Nature* proposed what is now known as Barnard's exact test, an unconditional alternative to Fisher's exact test for 2×2 contingency tables that he argued was more powerful and less conservative. He formalized the method in a 1947 paper in *Biometrika*.

After the war, Barnard went to Imperial College London as a lecturer, becoming a reader in 1948 and professor of mathematical statistics in 1954. In 1966 he moved to the newly created University of Essex, from which he retired in 1975. His most influential theoretical contributions concerned the foundations of statistical inference. His 1949 paper first articulated the likelihood principle — the idea that all evidence from an observation is contained in the likelihood function — which he considered his best work. He introduced the concept of conjugate prior distributions, now a basic building block of Bayesian analysis that appears in virtually every Bayesian paper. He also developed the pivotal approach to inference and was an early advocate of Monte Carlo testing methods.

Barnard served as president of three professional societies: the Operational Research Society (1962–1964), the Institute of Mathematics and its Applications (1970–1971), and the Royal Statistical Society (1971–1972). He was elected a Fellow of the American Statistical Association in 1961.

Barnard's story is notable in the history of statistics precisely because of how different it is from so many of his contemporaries. He came from the working class, not the professional elite. He was a committed anti-fascist and labor activist at a time when many of the field's founding figures were enthusiastic eugenicists. He spent his career advancing the mathematical foundations of inference and practical quality control — not racial science. He died on 30 July 2002.
