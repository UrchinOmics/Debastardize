### Francis Galton (1822–1911)

**Methods:** Regression toward the mean, correlation coefficient (precursor to Pearson's *r*), Galton board, composite photography, percentile/quartile/decile terminology, the biometric approach to heredity.

Francis Galton was born on February 16, 1822, in Birmingham, England, into a wealthy Quaker family. He was the half-cousin of Charles Darwin — they shared a grandfather, Erasmus Darwin. He studied medicine briefly at Birmingham General Hospital and King's College London, then mathematics at Trinity College, Cambridge, but took no degree of distinction. When his father died in 1844, leaving him a substantial inheritance, he abandoned his studies and spent several years traveling.

Between 1850 and 1852, Galton mounted a self-financed expedition through Damaraland and Ovampoland in what is now Namibia. He published an account of his travels as *Narrative of an Explorer in Tropical South Africa* (1853). The book is saturated with racism. Galton treated the African peoples he encountered as objects of study rather than as people. In one widely cited episode, he used a sextant to measure the figure of a Khoikhoi woman from a distance, treating her body as a geographic feature to be surveyed. He later wrote, "I saw enough of savage races, to give me material to think about all the rest of my life." The Royal Geographical Society awarded him a gold medal for this expedition.

The publication of Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* in 1859 transformed Galton's career. He became consumed by the idea that human ability was inherited and could be improved through selective breeding. In *Hereditary Genius* (1869), he analyzed the family trees of prominent men and argued that eminence ran in families because of biological inheritance, not social advantage. In a chapter titled "The Comparative Worth of Different Races," he constructed an explicit racial hierarchy, writing that "the average intellectual standard of the negro race is some two grades below our own." He claimed that "the white traveller almost invariably holds his own in their presence" and that the ancient Athenians represented the highest level of human ability ever achieved — a level they lost by interbreeding with lesser peoples. The methodology was circular, the data were anecdotal, and the conclusions were predetermined.

In *Inquiries into Human Faculty and Its Development* (1883), Galton coined the term "eugenics," from the Greek for "well born." He defined it as "the science of improving stock, which is by no means confined to questions of judicious mating, but which, especially in the case of man, takes cognisance of all influences that tend in however remote a degree to give to the more suitable races or strains of blood a better chance of prevailing speedily over the less suitable than they otherwise would have had." He sorted the British population into classes based on perceived ability and health, analyzed the likelihood of each class producing "desirable" offspring, and argued that the "fittest" classes should be encouraged to breed while the "unfit" should be discouraged from reproducing.

In 1904, Galton delivered the lecture "Eugenics: Its Definition, Scope and Aims" to the Sociological Society in London. He told the audience that the aim of eugenics was "to cause the useful classes in the community to contribute more than their proportion to the next generation." The lecture was widely publicized and served as a catalyst for the formation of eugenics societies across Europe and the United States.

Galton was not merely a theorist. In 1907, he helped establish the Eugenics Education Society in London. In a January 1907 letter to Karl Pearson, he wrote: "except by sterilisation I cannot yet see any way of checking the produce of the unfit who are allowed their liberty and are below the reach of moral control." He advocated for institutionalization and marriage restrictions for those he classified as "feeble-minded." While Galton himself did not live to see the forced sterilization programs that his movement inspired — Indiana enacted the first compulsory sterilization law in 1907, and by the 1930s over thirty U.S. states had followed — the intellectual framework was his.

Galton's statistical contributions were real and foundational. He discovered regression toward the mean through his studies of hereditary stature, observing that the children of exceptionally tall or short parents tended to be closer to the population average. He independently developed the concept of correlation in 1888, calling it the "index of correlation" and symbolizing it with the letter *r* (from "reversion"). He coined the terms "percentile," "quartile," and "decile." He invented the Galton board (quincunx) to demonstrate the normal distribution. He pioneered the use of composite photography. He developed the first scientific system for classifying fingerprints. Karl Pearson later formalized and extended Galton's statistical ideas into the correlation coefficient and regression analysis that are used today. None of these contributions were independent of his eugenic program. Galton developed statistical tools *because* he wanted to measure and rank human populations.

In his will, Galton bequeathed the residue of his estate — approximately £40,000, equivalent to roughly £4.75 million today — to University College London for "the establishment and endowment of a Professorship" to be known as "The Galton Professorship of Eugenics." He stipulated that Karl Pearson should be the first holder. Pearson accepted and became the Francis Galton Professor of Eugenics, a title he held until 1933. Galton also left UCL his personal collection, archive, and the institutional infrastructure of what became the Galton Laboratory. The professorship was not renamed to "Human Genetics" until 1963.

Galton was knighted in 1909. He died on January 17, 1911, in Haslemere, Surrey.

In June 2020, UCL removed Galton's name from its buildings. The Galton Lecture Theatre became Lecture Theatre 115. The decision followed a formal Inquiry into the History of Eugenics at UCL, which documented how Galton's bequest had embedded eugenic research into the university's institutional structure for over a century.

Francis Galton did not stumble into eugenics late in life. He built the field from the ground up. He named it. He defined it. He funded its institutional home. He provided the statistical tools and the intellectual framework that others — Karl Pearson, Ronald Fisher, Charles Davenport, and the architects of forced sterilization programs worldwide — used to sort human beings into categories of worth. The global eugenics movement of the twentieth century was, in a direct and traceable way, his project.
