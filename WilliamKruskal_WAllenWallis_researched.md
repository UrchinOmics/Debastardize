### William Kruskal (1919–2005) and W. Allen Wallis (1912–1998)

**Methods:** Kruskal–Wallis one-way analysis of variance (H test), a nonparametric method for testing whether samples originate from the same distribution, published in their 1952 paper "Use of Ranks in One-Criterion Variance Analysis."

William Henry Kruskal was born on October 10, 1919, in New York City to a Jewish family. His father was a fur wholesaler; his mother, Lillian Rose Vorhaus Kruskal Oppenheimer, later became a well-known promoter of origami on early television. Two of his brothers, Joseph and Martin Kruskal, also became prominent mathematicians and physicists. Kruskal earned bachelor's and master's degrees in mathematics from Harvard in 1940 and 1941, and completed his Ph.D. at Columbia in 1955 under Henry Scheffé and Howard Levene.

During World War II, Kruskal worked as a civilian at the U.S. Naval Proving Ground in Dahlgren, Virginia, on aviation ordnance — an experience that drew him into statistics. He joined the University of Chicago as an instructor in 1950 and spent his entire career there, rising to full professor. Beyond the Kruskal–Wallis test, Kruskal made important contributions to the understanding of measures of association, the role of statistics in public policy, and the foundations of statistical inference. He edited the *Annals of Mathematical Statistics* from 1958 to 1961, served as president of the Institute of Mathematical Statistics in 1971 and of the American Statistical Association in 1982, and received the ASA's Founders Award in 1992. He was appointed by President Nixon to the Commission on Federal Statistics in 1970, and became the first chairman of the National Research Council's Committee on National Statistics, a position he held until 1978. Much of his later work focused on census underenumeration, statistical confidentiality, and the responsible use of statistics in public controversy. He died on April 21, 2005.

Wilson Allen Wallis was born on November 5, 1912, in Philadelphia. He earned a bachelor's degree in psychology from the University of Minnesota in 1932 and pursued graduate work in economics at the University of Chicago, where he formed lifelong friendships with future Nobel laureates Milton Friedman and George Stigler. The three were part of the so-called "Room Seven Gang" — a group of young free-market economists who gathered in the economics department and went on to define the Chicago school of economics.

From 1942 to 1946, Wallis directed the Statistical Research Group at Columbia University under the Office of Scientific Research and Development, recruiting an extraordinary group of young statisticians, economists, and mathematicians who contributed to the Allied war effort. After the war, he joined the University of Chicago's Graduate School of Business, eventually becoming its dean in 1956, where he and James H. Lorie developed the "Chicago Approach to Business Education" — a model emphasizing foundational scientific knowledge that became widely influential in graduate business programs.

In 1962, Wallis became president of the University of Rochester, a position he held until 1970, when he transitioned to chancellor (serving as chief executive until 1975 and as chancellor until 1982). He transformed Rochester from a regional institution into a nationally prominent research university, raising its annual budget from $33 million to $200 million, increasing the faculty by 37 percent, and doubling graduate enrollment. His tenure was not without friction: in 1967, he contracted with the Department of the Navy to manage the Center for Naval Analyses, a military research operation based in Arlington, Virginia. As the Vietnam War intensified, both faculty and students demanded that the university sever its ties with the center. A faculty group called the Committee of Concern formally questioned Wallis about the arrangement, and the campus chapter of Students for a Democratic Society called on him to explain the program publicly. Wallis refused to end the relationship, a decision that embittered many faculty members.

Wallis was a committed free-market conservative throughout his career. He served as treasurer of the Mont Pelerin Society from 1948 to 1954, an international organization of classical liberal intellectuals founded by Friedrich Hayek. He advised Presidents Eisenhower, Nixon, Ford, and Reagan on economic policy. Under Reagan, he served as Under Secretary of State for Economic Affairs from 1982 to 1989, coordinating U.S. international economic programs under his longtime friend Secretary of State George Shultz. Shultz called Wallis "our truth teller." After his government service, the University of Rochester established the W. Allen Wallis Institute of Political Economy in his honor.

It is worth noting that Wallis used his statistical expertise to critique the misuse of statistics by eugenicists, pointing out that Charles Davenport had selectively used standard deviations rather than coefficients of variation to manufacture support for the claim that racial "hybrids" showed greater — and therefore "disharmonious" — variability. Wallis concluded that Davenport's generalization was, at best, a moot point and, at worst, simply false.

Neither Kruskal nor Wallis is associated with eugenics, scientific racism, or the other forms of harm that characterize many of the founders of modern statistics. Kruskal was a careful methodologist and public servant who championed the responsible use of government statistics. Wallis was a politically active conservative economist and university administrator whose statistical work helped debunk eugenic claims, though his free-market ideology and his willingness to maintain military research ties during the Vietnam era were contentious in their own right. The Kruskal–Wallis test, one of the most widely used nonparametric methods in science, carries no such baggage.
